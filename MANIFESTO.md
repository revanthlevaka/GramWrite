# GramWrite — Brand Manifesto

---

## The Problem We Don't Talk About

Every screenwriting software ships with a grammar checker.
Every grammar checker was built for essays, emails, and cover letters.

None of them were built for the way screenwriters actually write.

They flag `He runs.` as a fragment.
They underline `CLOSE ON — her hands.` as a sentence error.
They want you to turn `She's gone.` into `She has gone.`

Screenwriters learn to ignore grammar tools.
Or worse — they internalize them, and the prose gets cautious.

GramWrite exists because this is a solvable problem.

---

## Our Belief

> A good editor is invisible.

The best script doctors in Hollywood don't call you while you're drafting.
They don't leave sticky notes on your monitor every time you type a fragment.
They read. They wait. And when you come to them — they have something useful.

That's what GramWrite is.

---

## What GramWrite Is

GramWrite is a **local AI sidecar** — a small piece of software that lives beside your screenwriting tool.

It watches. It learns the structure of your document.
It understands that `INT. OFFICE — NIGHT` is not a sentence to correct.
It knows that `SARAH` in all-caps is a character, not a typo.
It respects that `Blood on concrete. Wind.` is intentional.

When it finds a real error — a genuine grammar mistake in your dialogue, a misspelling in your action lines — it glows. Gently. In the corner.

You can ignore it forever.
Or you can click it.

---

## What GramWrite Is Not

**Not a rewriter.**
GramWrite does not suggest better phrasing. It does not offer alternatives to your dialogue. It does not have opinions about your story. The moment it tries to rewrite your voice, we have failed.

**Not a cloud service.**
Your screenplay contains your most private creative work. It is not training data. It is not a product to be analysed by a company you've never heard of. GramWrite runs entirely on your machine — on hardware you own, via models you've downloaded.

**Not a feature dashboard.**
The interface is a dot. That is intentional. Software that respects your attention earns your trust.

---

## The Rules We Enforce on Ourselves

1. **Never rewrite.** Grammar and spelling only. Nothing else.

2. **Never interrupt.** The tool speaks only when spoken to. No popups. No badges. No notifications. Only a glow.

3. **Never leak.** Zero network calls to external servers. Zero telemetry. Zero accounts. The architecture enforces the privacy pledge — it is not a setting.

4. **Never break Fountain.** Sluglines are sacred. Character names are sacred. Transitions are sacred. The grammar checker must understand screenplay structure before it corrects anything.

5. **Never add weight.** GramWrite should boot in under two seconds. Inference should complete in under 100ms. The memory footprint should be smaller than the app it sits beside.

## The Screenwriter We're Building For

She's been writing for twelve years.
She's had two features produced, one that went nowhere, and a pilot she's still proud of.
She writes in Fade In because she likes the feel of it.
She doesn't want AI to write for her.
She doesn't want suggestions about her story structure.
She doesn't want a subscription.

She just wants to know when she typed `from I` instead of `from me` in the middle of a long session at 1am.

That's it. That's the whole product.

---

## On Open Source

GramWrite is open source because it has to be.

A tool that reads your creative work, accesses your OS accessibility layer, and processes your text with an AI — that tool must be auditable. Privacy claims are worthless without code.

We also believe the best tools for writers are built *with* writers. Every contributor who improves text extraction reliability, adds support for a new app, or fixes a bug on Windows — they're building something that matters to real people doing real creative work.

## What Success Looks Like

A writer finishes a draft.
She never thought about grammar while she was writing it.
She doesn't remember the last time she clicked the green dot.
But when she exports the script and reads it back, the dialogue is clean.

She didn't notice GramWrite helping her.
That's exactly right.
